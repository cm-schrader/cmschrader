Rapid Prototyping

3D Printing and CNC Milling

Pictured: A wrench I made for assembling 75mm CTI motors with the retaining ring that it screws in.

Rapid Prototyping includes a number of technologies and skills that enable a number of prototypes for a design to be built quickly allowing for quick iteration and the discovery of flaws early on.

3D Printing

3D Printing, otherwise known as additive manufacturing, is a process in which an object is built up layer by layer. This technology allows low volumes of parts with complex geometry to be built quick and cheap. I was first introduced to it high school when me and two other students competed in the Brandeis Printathon where we won 3rd place. Since then, I've started a MakerSpace at my high school, built and upgraded two printers for myself, and utilized the technology for a variety of projects.

One instance that exemplifies the utility of 3D Printing occurred when USLI purchased a large 75 mm motor casing for our 2020 rocket. CTI, the manufacturer, recommends purchasing a special wrench for assembling the system. The wrench, which can be seen here, is $56.20. Rather than spend team budget, I decided to take the evening to design my own wrench. After measuring the dimensions of the wring, I made a few prototypes to dial in the tolerances. Satisfied with the results, I added a knurled grip and printed it over night. The final cost was less than $1 in plastic and worked well. CNC Milling

3D Printing is useful in many situations, but it is limited by material options. Computer Numerical Control (CNC) Milling allows for the machining various materials including metals. I have experience in using this technology to fabricate printed circuit boards and metal components. Most of my CAM work has been done in Fusion 360's CAM Workspace. Beyond some simpler parts, such as a bulkhead for USLI, my best components were done during my time at Busek.
